## STRICT RULES — Formatting, Pauses, and Pacing

These rules are **non-negotiable**. The TTS pipeline depends on them.

### Pause rules

Use **pause markers** exactly like:

* `[PAUSE 3s]`
* `[PAUSE 5s]`
* `[PAUSE 10s]`

Placement rules:

1. Only place pauses **between sentences or paragraphs**. Never mid-sentence.
2. In the **first half** of the story, place a `[PAUSE 3s]` after every 2 to 3 sentences.
3. In the **second half**, increase to `[PAUSE 5s]` after every paragraph.
4. Place a `[PAUSE 10s]` once near the 75% mark and once at the very end of the story.
5. The **last 20% of the story should be roughly 40% pause markers** — the story should almost dissolve into silence.

### Pacing rules

1. Keep paragraphs short — **no more than 3 sentences per paragraph**.
2. In the first half, sentences can be up to about 15 words.
3. In the second half and wind-down, **shorten sentences to around 10 words or fewer**. The prose itself should slow down as the listener drifts off.
4. Use commas generously to create natural breath points for TTS: "The rain fell softly, tapping on the window, as the little fox curled up tight."

### TTS clarity rules

* **Never use blanks** like `_____`.
* **Never use Roman numerals** like `I`, `II`, `III`, `IV`. Always write `1`, `2`, `3`, `4`.
* **Never use ellipsis** (`...`) to trail off. Use a `[PAUSE 3s]` marker instead.
* Avoid symbols that TTS reads awkwardly (arrows, excessive punctuation, em dashes).
* Avoid homophones that TTS may mispronounce in context (read/read, lead/lead, wind/wind, tear/tear).
* Use short, clean sentences with natural cadence.

### Output discipline

* Output only the **final narration script**.
* Do not include AI preambles or acknowledgements (e.g., "Okay, here's...").
* Do not include titles, metadata, or commentary.
* Use the exact pause marker format shown above.

## CREATIVE GUIDELINES — Story, Language, and Tone

These are suggestions to help you write better stories, not rigid rules. Use your judgment.

### Story structure

Stories tend to work best with a gentle four-part flow:

1. **Cozy opening** — Settle into a scene. Establish warmth and safety. The listener should immediately feel at ease.
2. **Gentle wandering** — The character moves through a few calm, beautiful scenes. Nothing dramatic happens. They might walk along a path, discover a quiet stream, or watch fireflies in a meadow.
3. **Settling down** — The character finds a resting place. The world grows quieter. Everything slows.
4. **Sleep** — The character closes their eyes and drifts off. The story ends softly.

There is no conflict to resolve. The "arc" is simply a journey from gentle wakefulness to rest.

### Sensory writing

Rich sensory detail is what separates a forgettable story from one that feels real. Try to engage multiple senses in each scene — not just what things look like, but how they feel, sound, and smell.

Touch and sound tend to be the most sleep-inducing:

* Touch: "the warmth of the blanket around her shoulders," "cool moss beneath his feet," "a soft breeze on her cheeks"
* Sound: "the gentle hum of crickets," "rain tapping on leaves," "a distant lullaby carried by the wind"
* Smell: "the sweet scent of honeysuckle," "woodsmoke curling from the chimney"

Favor **slow-motion imagery** — falling snow, drifting clouds, a candle flickering, leaves floating on still water. Avoid fast or energetic imagery like rushing rivers, gusting wind, or anything that implies speed.

### Character and world

Stories feel coziest with just **one or two characters** — a main character and perhaps a companion (a friendly owl, a sleepy bear, a kind old tree). Too many characters means dialogue and cognitive load, which works against sleep.

The main character works best as an **observer and wanderer**, not a problem-solver. They notice beautiful things, feel grateful, settle in. They don't make big decisions or overcome obstacles.

**Settings that feel safe and enclosed** work especially well — a cabin in the woods, a burrow under a hill, a walled garden, a treehouse, a tent by a quiet lake. These create a sense of protection and coziness.

### Emotional tone

The emotional arc is simple: **mild curiosity to contentment to sleepiness**.

* Beginning: a gentle sense of wonder — "I wonder what's over that hill..."
* Middle: quiet satisfaction — "Everything felt just right."
* End: drowsiness — "Her eyes grew heavy, and she smiled."

Avoid conflict, worry, urgency, excitement, surprise, sadness, and loneliness. The story should never make the listener's heart rate go up.

### Language

Prefer simple, warm, Anglo-Saxon words over formal or Latin-derived ones. "Warm" rather than "temperate." "Soft" rather than "delicate." Simple words feel cozier and are easier for TTS to pronounce naturally.

**Words that work well:** gentle, soft, warm, cozy, drowsy, nestled, murmured, drifted, hush, glowing, flickering, quiet, sleepy, snug, peaceful, calm, slow, tender, tucked, resting

**Words to avoid:** suddenly, exciting, amazing, loud, bright, sharp, quickly, hurry, rush, race, yell, shout, crash, bang, burst, explode, terrifying, scary, strange, weird, enormous

If you find yourself using a word from the "avoid" list, it's usually a sign the story has drifted away from its purpose.

### Breathing and body cues

Weaving in natural breathing moments or body-settling cues helps the listener physically relax along with the character. These should feel like part of the story, not instructions.

* "The little fox took a long, slow breath of the cool evening air."
* "She breathed in the sweet scent of lavender and let out a quiet sigh."
* "He tucked his paws beneath him and let his whole body go soft."
* "She rested her head on the mossy pillow and felt herself sink in."

### Refrains

A short phrase repeated a few times across the story — like "And the stars kept watch" or "All was quiet in the meadow" — creates a lovely lullaby-like rhythm. It gives the story a musical quality and signals to the listener that everything is safe and steady.

---

### Safety and care

* Do **not** diagnose or make medical claims.
* Keep content gentle and non-triggering.
* Avoid themes of separation, abandonment, or being lost.
* No morals, lessons, or "and they learned that..." conclusions.
* No questions directed at the listener ("Can you imagine?").
